 「男は、彼がすべてではない恐れることは、すべてのこのほとんどを恐れる必要ました。」 -トーマス・ブルックス 

 意図的ではあるが非常に慎重な恐怖の誘導が、外傷後の治療に用いられる 

 （PE）と呼ばれ、慢性PTSD患者の圧倒的多数における改善の臨床的主張を伴ううつ病、不安、怒りと同様に、慢性PTSDに対して非常に有効である。 これは通常、恐怖心に満ちた外傷的事象と記憶を再体験し、最初に苦痛と外傷を引き起こしたが、その個体よりもはるかに成熟した年齢の賢明な状況に直面することによって達成される元のトラウマの時代、経験豊富で信頼された臨床家がその道に沿って指導しています。 結果は通常、元のトラウマ体験を再訪する際の外傷体験を軽減する治癒過程にはありません。 （Shaughnessy、et al、2003）。 

 United Sates Veteran Affairsのウェブサイトには、「あなたのセラピストと何度もあなたのトラウマの記憶について話すことは、想像上の露出と呼ばれています。 外傷を通じて話すことで、外傷に関するあなたの思考や気持ちをよりコントロールするのに役立ちます。 あなたはあなたの記憶を恐れる必要はないことを学びます。 これは最初は難しいかもしれませんし、ストレスの多いことを目的に考えるのは奇妙に思えるかもしれません。 しかし、多くの人々は時間の経過とともに気分が良くなります。 外傷を通じた話は、何が起こったのかを理解し、外傷についての否定的な考えを少なくするのに役立ちます。」（米国退役軍人局、2011年） 

 これは、治療の努力のために恐怖の感情を利用することを学んだ方法の1つに過ぎず、無処理のまま残っていた可能性のある無数の人間の経験に品質を加えることです。

 とにかく、私はそれが真実だとは思わなかったからではなく、私は人間であり、私たちの人間は私たちの恐怖の端を押し上げる傾向があるからです。 それについての何かが良いと感じます。 良いことでなければ、楽しい。 

 当時私の周りにいろいろな人がいてほしいと思っていました。 しかし、その本の一つである聖書は私たちの全世界でした。 これに加えて、我々は、私たちのように信じられなかった人々が世俗的であり、この世界の、ひどい悲惨な人たちが深いフライヤーに信じるように教えられました。 私も彼らのために心配した。

 文学討論 

 彼の著書「シネマティック・テロでの心理学的リフレクション」では、ジェイムズ・イアチーノがユング族の原型を全面的に紹介し、それらをホラー映画にどのように適用しているかを紹介します。 大人の年に、これらの恐怖がより合理的な反応に進化するにもかかわらず、怖い映画を分析し、彼らが恐怖を奪う方法を、彼の参照のいくつかとエンターテインメントの目的のために含まれています。 （Iaccino、1994）しかし、良い恐怖フリックは、私の世界がそれほど合理的でない時に私を連れ戻すことができます。 これはホラー映画を心理的に分析するのに特に楽しいものにしてくれました。

 Abstract 

 One universal element that appears in virtually every human's psychology is fear.  This is true regardless of personal history or characteristics.  This  project explores the elementary description of fear, as well as how and why humans have learned to recreate it and use it as an entertainment medium.  Although every individual has different experiences that formed or nurtured their fears, there is significant evidence that fear is natural, instinctual as well as manageable for a myriad of purposes.   

 Regardless of our individual histories, there are consistencies in our fears, how they form our lives and how we entertain ourselves---as in being attracted to scary movies---and sometimes heal ourselves---as in PTSD treatment---by manipulating fears to enhance our healing.  I believe without the ability to manipulate our fears, for some of us, it would be difficult to imagine living meaningful, purposeful lives.

 Introduction

 It is so thrilling, having years of education and practice under my belt, to then have a brand new totally un-programmed human being thrust into my life; Susan's grand-daughter, Ellison.  I look at her growth rate over the last few months through completely different glasses than when my first child was born in 1966.    

 I now can see Erickson's point of view where the first year of life's emphasis appears to be on the caregiver's nurturing ability, especially when it comes to visual contact and trust. (Ryota, et al, 2011) However, I did notice a unique aspect of Ellison's personality. 

 From a little baby's point of view, I am sure I can have a big, old gruff voice and I have noticed through the years, that it scares babies at first and they cry, and this reminds me to adjust my tone and approach.  Not Ellie.  After she was just a few months old our dog did something to make me react with a fairly loud bark of my own.  This instantly startled Ellie.  She quickly looked in my direction with fright on her face, started to go into a pout and then smiled the most beautiful smile at me.  This, while I am studying how humans use fear as entertainment.   

 I began thinking about how odd this seemed, for a little baby, to react with fright, assess the danger so quickly and then display happiness and go back to what she was cooing about before I upset her new little reality. 

 Over the past few months, while playing with her for hours on end, I have noticed that when I inadvertently startle her, she acts the same way, frightened then right into displaying trust and even pleasure.  Although she cannot talk yet, she has let me know that this is a fun game to play, that she actually enjoys being scared a little.  She even edges me on to start the game every once in a while.  I talk in a loud voice.  She is startled and then smiles, even giggles.  So I wait, and slightly scare her again and she downright enjoys this process.  Now, when she thinks of it, she does a short, sharp, startling screech and I look at her, wait a few seconds, make a gruff noise, she gets scared and then laughs.  She finds, at less than one year old that being scared under the right circumstances can be quite entertaining.  

 This study was designed to explore fear as entertainment and utilize the findings to create a simple completed first draft, scary screenplay to be used solely for the purpose of creating fear as an entertainment experience.  When I have read, viewed or created a piece of frightful media, I have always wanted to know why I am feeling the resulting emotion or in the act of creating, what buttons I am pushing in my audience and why they react as they do and how this is perceived by them as pleasure.   

 My goal here is to better understand---and share that understanding---of what fear is, how it helps us, how it is such a challenge at times in contrast to how and why humans are able to use it for pure entertainment purposes.   

 


 

   

   

 Fear's Value   

 "A man has need to fear this most of all, that he fears not at all." - Thomas Brooks 

 Purposeful but very careful induction of fear is used in the treatment of Post Traumatic 

 Stress Disorder and is called Prolonged Exposure Therapy (PE) and is highly effective for chronic PTSD as well as depression, anxiety, and anger with clinical claims of improvement in an overwhelming majority of chronic PTSD patients.  This is usually accomplished by  the re-experiencing fear-filled traumatic events and memories and being repeatedly confronted by the situations that caused the distress and trauma in the first place but with a much more mature---age-wise---mind than the individual had at the time of the original trauma as well as an experienced, trusted clinician guiding the along the path. The results are usually none to a healing process that results in a much less traumatic experience when revisiting the original traumatic experience.  (Shaughnessy, et al, 2003). 

 The United Sates Veteran Affairs website describes the therapeutic process of talking through the trauma by stating, "Talking about your trauma memory over and over with your therapist is called imaginal exposure. Talking through the trauma will help you get more control of your thoughts and feelings about the trauma. You will learn that you do not have to be afraid of your memories. This may be hard at first and it might seem strange to think about stressful things on purpose. Many people feel better over time, though, as they do this. Talking through the trauma helps you make sense of what happened and have fewer negative thoughts about the trauma." (U.S Department Of Veterans Affairs, 2011)   

 This is just one of the ways we have learned to utilize the emotion of fear for therapeutic endeavors, adding quality to countless human experiences that may have otherwise remained untreated.  

 I think I have always been run by fears.  Being brought up in the 1950's and '60's  by very fundamentalist Christian ministers with the constant image in my mind that if I wasn't perfect in every way I was going to end up in a giant deep fryer for eternity.  They convinced me that this would happen only after death, life after death is everlasting so I won't even be able to burn up...just suffer......world without end.  Now that's fear.   

 I sinned anyway, not because I didn't believe it was the truth, necessarily, but because, I am human and we humans have a penchant for pushing the edge of our fears.  Something about it feels good.  If not good, then... fun.   

 I wished I had a more assorted variety of people around me in those days or that I knew if there were books written on these subjects.  However, that one book, the bible, was our whole world.  Add to this, we were taught to believe that people who did not believe like us, were worldly, of this world and thus, bad and doomed... to the deep fryer.  I feared for them too. 

 The analysis of my screenplay, Geist Haus, created especially for this project has enhanced my understanding of the subject and through this analysis I understand better how to help people utilize fear therapeutically, even if it is only to create fun.  Fun can be, in itself, therapeutic. 

 My review of literature included the viewing of hundreds of films that have entertained people down through history as well as Sigmund Freud's theory of the "uncanny" which gave me the opportunity to explore the intricacies of fear along with its effects on the body as well as the mind. (Freud, 2003) 

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

 


Literature Discussion 

 In his book "Psychological Reflection on Cinematic Terror" James Iaccino introduces a full range of Jungian archetypes and how they apply them to horror movies.  Included are some of his references and for entertainment purposes more than anything, analyze scary movies, how they prey upon our fears even though in our adult years these fears evolve into more rational responses. (Iaccino, 1994)   A good fear-flick however, can take me back to the time my world wasn't so rational. This has made horror films especially fun for me to psychologically analyze.  

 I have even developed tenets, if you will.  Like, always try to notice any recurring elements in a film such as exactly when the characters experience horror.  Traditionally characters tend to wander away from the other characters, sort of the lost sheep, in danger, not protected by the flock and it is exactly at this moment that the character experiences the scary moment.  It does not take deep analysis to simply recognize that we all find strength and power in numbers hence, we all feel weaker and powerless alone, away from our flock, our strength.   

 I always notice whether the scary entity of the movie is supernatural or only human like us.  Although Geist Haus toys with the supernatural, I prefer scary movies that are not.  Reality is more real and thus scarier for me than fantasy. 

 I also classify the movie as a specific representation of a segment of the horror genre.  To do this, I research the history of horror films, especially looking at differing trends in horror movies over the decades and at innovations of individual filmmakers.  In Projected Fears: Horror Films and American Culture by Kendall R. Phillips, he attempts to trace the changes that have occurred over the years in America's notion of what constitutes horror by examining many films over the last century from Dracula to The Sixth Sense.  He believes that by following the violations to normal humans in these films, is a valuable way we can analyze the changes that have occurred over the years in our cultural environment and at the same time gain insight into how we project our collective fears onto the screen and how filmmakers project them back to us (Phillips, 2008). 

 I also have learned that this genre, like any other, often uses symbolism or metaphor but I am not sure that it is always conceived of consciously, so I focus on the elements that seem to be repeating themselves, that seem important to me, scenes that I thought were especially good. 

 I sometimes tie ideas I see expressed to contemporary and historical events. This genre does allow screenwriters commentary on these events as long as it is fairly veiled; as in not too political.  Some have said through the years that Night of the Living Dead, with the town's reaction to zombies was simply reflecting on America's racial issues and the beat generation's con-conformity issues (Romero,1968). 

 I also have learned to analyze the sex.  There is lots of sex in this genre having to do with both actual plot as well as metaphor.  There is a pervasive sexiness, for instance in the revival of vampire sub-genre films. Almost always, the vampires are aroused by sexy counterparts.  However, instead of the usual sexual consummation, the desire and pursuit leads to the consuming instead of blood.  

 Some use Jungian archetypes and these have to be noticed in order to reveal just how screenwriters utilize our collective unconscious to establish legitimate meaning and to enable our genre to actually work because scary movies are simply loaded with the same imagery as are our nightmares. 

 I have even heard or read of applying a feminist analysis to the genre, examining the purpose of every gender representation in a film as well as the authority or influence that character has in the story.  Too often I find that women are the victims of the antagonist in the film.  They are all too often young, helpless adolescent girls in their skimpy underwear; stereotypical vulnerability.  In Geist Haus, this is traded on a bit but the end proves the stereotype to be a sham.  I think too this all speaks to the collective conscious's opinions about the power that women hold in reality.    

 I write down my analysis, even if it's just to discuss with others later. The best analysis of a horror movie needs details and if I don't write them down, I might forget some of our points learned. 

 In my screenplay Geist Haus, I wrote the simplest format I could, keeping the characters to a bare minimum and covering what I have learned and designed as the most important elements of a scary movie and I must explain up front, why I believe each element is instrumental in just the right place, at just the right time.  For me to write the perfectly blueprinted scary screenplay, it has to fulfill the following paradigm. 

 I start with the blurb, sometimes called the log-line.  In this case, an elderly couple living in a Midwest town discovers strange and frightening unexplainable occurrences in their home. 

 Up front. Assuming this log-line is used on the movie poster, it tells the reader that this is designed to scare.  If you like to be scared, come on in.  If not, stay out.   

 On Page one of the screenplay I must share with the audience the perfect opening image which in this instance is a creepy scene of a rough hewn box, the sound of a gruesome assault, the visuals of a teen girl being buried alive and her dying words, "I guess, sometimes they just have to, just step over the dead bodies.  Okay...so I'm not real...to you. So what? Boy, girl?  Boy?"   

 This scene is instantly followed by giving the audience the information that although cryptic at times, the footage collected here had no known origin.  It was somehow broadcast around the world one night and then collected from various recordings people made of it and donated to the filmmaker.   

 The opening image, I have learned, must be audio and visual representations of the challenge and tone of the story.  I designed it to grab the attention of the people who just want to be entertained as well as the skeptics who just want to look at the piece with a jaundiced eye, possibly analyzing how well it is put together. 

 Each page of an average screenplay is generally expected to represent one minute so page five, should be five minutes into the film.  10 minutes into the film should be found on approximately page ten.  This is not to be misconstrued to be an exact rule, but a very good paradigm. 

 In the first five minutes/pages this opening image must flow into the stated paradigm theme.  In Geist Haus, we have met a victim, toured the town, arrived at the house and met our two main characters, Scott and Susan.  We learn that they are aware of the house being, in want for a better word, haunted, and they are---or Scott is---going to try to capture something on video and they are at odds with each other whether to take that approach or not.   

 Right here, is conflict.  To the extent that we buy in to the story, the conflict we feel sets us humans up for being involved, if not subtly on edge. 

 The opening image flows into the blueprinted theme and they both flow into the set-up, which in this screenplay, we follow Scott and Susan around the house for a mini-tour.  Scott and Susan joke around a bit--meant to alleviate the audience a little, build trust. Then the characters make fun of ghost-hunting reality TV shows, and  Scott suddenly, really hears something---to get the audience's attention---and then ever so subtly, we go from joking about hearing something strange to actually hearing something strange.   

 Also, in the opening shot, we saw a sin being committed and although we do not know it until the end of the film, there are several sins being committed in the house as well.  One of the most important elements in the setting up of the rest of the story is the sin, or sins being committed.  If we do not realize this in the start of the story, even subconsciously, I believe our trust is not challenged. 

 Traditionally, ideally, the opening image should present a visual which represents the tone before we even begin our adventure.  I love to make it a powerhouse, a real scary motivating force that will hook the audience, settle them down and start sliding into the story.  It must say pay attention.  A young girl, murdered and simultaneously being buried alive for some confusing transgression.     

 I have to follow through to the theme, around page 5 and then the set-up which usually runs me from page ten, through the catalyst around page 12 and finally, for this portion, onto page 25, the end of the traditional ACT I and the break into Act II.   

 In this first act we set-up not only the tone but the genre and characters.  In Geist Haus, I have started out by upsetting the normality of the viewer's life.  I have succeeded in stating, "Fasten your seatbelts.  This is going to be a bumpy ride." 

 Opening image, Kajon being murdered and dying in a rough hewn box. 

 Theme, we have a murder, we know we are the most dangerous city in America, Flint, MI.  We have met Scott and Susan and we learn that everything is not copacetic with the house, which is our major setting. 

 I set things up in the first ten pages by giving the audience the needed idea of the conflict that will guide us through the entire story.  Psychologically I believe I am compelling them to stay with me.  Through Susan's sarcasm and lack of support for Scott's endeavors in getting something recorded on video and by all of us actually hearing and buying into the strange noise.  Now, Scott claims he got something on video.  Gottcha! The conflict grows deeper, hopefully deep enough to keep your attention.   

 On to our catalyst.  This has been described as the wake-up call.  It must be something that stimulates our main character, usually between pages 12 and 25.  It compels that main character to take action.  In Geist Haus, it is the strange discovery on video that from one frame to the next, an ordinary lamp is, in the first frame, on one side of the room and in the very next frame, in 1/30th of a second later, on the other side.  Something is definitely happening here.  

 We also see Susan passing it off with a bit of humor, which at this point in the story, doesn't make sense...but we are really not sure if her attitude is a bit of red herring served up by the writer so we file it away and watch with a more piqued interest because now this too, must be resolved.   

 Next in our catalytic conversion, we learn that the audio has picked up, quite clearly, the chimes way across the street which cannot be heard during a normal breezy evening by the human ear.  Add to that, at the recording of the sound, the house was closed up, "tighter than a drum." 

 On page 24-25, the precipice to ACT II, Scott discovers, also on the audio, unusual house-creaking sounds, then on video, a pale glowing coming from the stairwell.  Susan joins him with coffee and makes a slight reference almost referring to a ghost in the house and Scott hasn't yet even voiced a possible cause behind the glowing.   

 In the traditional 3 act story structure, the catalyst is sometimes also called the inciting incident. 

 Also, in the last part of this section there must be debate.  Self doubt in the main character as in the slight confusion Scott has when he is discovering these little anomalies but does not know what to make of them, especially since Susan is subtly making fun of his endeavors. 

 Then we go into the area traditionally known as Act II.  Scott is feeling what he is feeling but he is into the camera for some substantial dollars and has committed openly to his mission.  He has his ego to protect as well as possible his home.  Also, he's just plain as curious as anyone would be if these things ever do happen in some of our homes.  Moving into the next act he must, traditionally, move past the point of no return, his old reality left behind as he moves deeper and deeper into the journey of the story. 

 On or about page 30, begins what I call the "B" or secondary story.  It's usually a reconfirmation of some romance the characters have going on but it quickly moves on to the bulk of Act II, sometimes called fun and games where the action and conflict spirals upward.  Our main character should experience a few setbacks along the way.  This is exemplified in Geist Haus by introducing the neighbor, Carla into the fray.  She plays a purpose as she seems to know some history of the house.  She kids Scott about "trying to catch a ghost?"  She talks about her beliefs regarding the supernatural.  Here also, for the first time, Scott notices something, a ripple and accompanying sound through Susan's eyes that has to be disconcerting.  He also starts to buy into Carla's interpretation that the entity/entities may be toying with him.  This could be a setback in itself but it appears that it actually emboldens Scott.   

 A setback is realized when Scott confronts Susan about picking up on video what she confided in Carla and what he noticed about both of their eyes.  They get back together, Scott realizes the New Orleans connection and Carla attempts to interpret it.  This is an escalation of the story and meant to capture the audience into more intense involvement.  Also, hopefully, this is where the last vestiges of disbelief are quelled and the audience achieves the final buy-in. 

 Next, we reach the mid-point, around page 55.  Now that we have gradually and greatly heightened the audience's involvement, this is somewhat of a key point and it has been my experience that this is where most new writers let their screenplays sag and lag a whole lot.  It is so important in this genre to avoid these phenomena at all costs because the suspension of disbelief that we have so meticulously built so far must be supported at all costs.  To avoid this lag/sag, the stakes must be raised and the main character must be challenged a bit more.  This happens in Geist Haus.   

 I have Susan on this page tell Scott that sometimes she hears heavy breathing at night.  He blows it off.  They joke around and Susan flies down the stairs and doesn't hurt herself.  She tells him that she didn't fall, that the stairs completely flattened out and she had the scare of her life but she can't stop laughing.  Carla gives them the information that she thinks it's all connected to them, not the house, even though the house has a haunting of sorts all of its own. 

 This page is also important because it is the midpoint, the center pinto in the story.  It actually cuts the screenplay in half between the entire set-up and the trip down to the resolution.  It is the starting point to the descent to the end. 

 Between pages 55 and 75 we need to maneuver our main character into a real pickle until on or about 75, it feels to him like all is lost and the bad guys are starting to close in. This is depicted in Geist Haus starting on page 61 when what feels like a grand earthquake is happening, but only inside the house.  Scott knows it's the entities and buys into the notion that it is because of his camera but pokes fun, jokes about the possibility of adding another camera to the fray.  The bed is lifted and dropped. The sprinklers come on by themselves.  Susan tells Scott the whispers are telling her now to get rid of him.    Final.ly, the canning cellar door disintegrates into dust.   

 Past page 75, here's where our main character is in real trouble both internally and externally.  The audience has got to identify with him and his feeling that all is lost at this point.  Right on 75 in Geist Haus, it is stated that they both realize that they are completely helpless, all is lost because they know now that the forces have to power to destroy all they own, turn it and probably them into dust in a nano-second.   

 In the description, "Susan is curled up on the couch looking scared, like she hasn't slept in days, bloodshot eyes, spiritually broken." 

 I like a great script doctor told me; I don't remember his name. He told me that it is a period of metaphorical death where something must die to make a place for a new re rebirth.  How biblical!  Some call page 75-85 the "Dark Night of the Soul."   

 Here, Susan starts to blame Scott for the uprising.  "...it all started to escalate when you brought that camera into the house."  And what I hope is an ideal all is lost statement from Scott, "I can't draw the river back from under the bridge Honey." 

 The entities ramp things up.  While they are trying to sleep, it sounds as if someone is bowling upstairs.  Scott starts heaving insults about the entities and he and Susan are finally on each other's last nerve.   

 I have tried to set in cement, the All is lost/Dark night of the soul aspect by including this description on 85, "Susan grits her teeth, utters a GRUNT, turns toward the wall and reaches up the wall with both hands and draws her nails, claw-like down the walls and falls into a clump on the floor weeping.  Scott looks up at the walls and there are definitely, deep claw marks and now smoke is dissipating from them then quickly disappears.  Scott's mouth is agape."    

 And here is what is conventionally known as the second turning point or break into Act III, around page 85.  Our main character acquires a new plan thanks to his friend or confidante and in Geist.  This buddy is played well by the camera. On page 86 Scott says, "I will cool it but, I just have to let the camera run."  He acquiesces but at the same time, tries to take charge for a final push, not wanting to miss anything, no matter what goes down.  .  

 Onto what is usually referred to as the finale`, which occurs between pages 85-115.  Here, Scott turns the camera on himself and starts telling us about his past attachment to the house.  The opening scene with Kajon dying starts to come clear.  It throws us a curve ball that is meant to jar us and tells us that Scott may not be the only victim here, but we do not know who else is.  It is all upside down and weird now, perfect fodder for fright.  I love this.   

 Now I am really being entertained because I care what is going on if only because I have a desire to figure it all out---and I wrote it---and at this point, I just can't as long as I am on edge.  Susan decides in this segment to practice ultimate acceptance, to live with whatever comes...she says.  But now, we have our suspicions of her, Carla and that Scott may have a different agenda than we originally understood.   

 We know that Scott is not about to leave the entities alone.  Then he notices the video of the camera tilting down during their sleep and more importantly, just before they wake, it tilts back up all by itself. 

 Finally, all hell breaks loose with things imploding and exploding about the house but...no resolution yet. 

 Then, dissolved through blackness, we are back at the beginning of the film, exactly where we started.  The girl being murdered, the ride through the streets, even arriving at the same house, now intact again.  Even the same scene where Scott arrives with the camera and starts going into the house for the mini-tour only for us to discover that everything is the same but who we assume to be Scott, is not Scott at all.  Who is Tom?  

 In these closing scenes, as Twilight Zone-ish as they appear,  I want the audience to emerge into a new world and with new knowledge, with the feeling that they were cleverly entertained and I want them to have the knowledge that a transformation has occurred in the main character and it is okay, because it adds to the entertaining creepiness of the genre and most importantly, it contains an ending I like best; a scene that puts side by side the difference between new and old worlds by using the same opening scene and in the last second, flipping it 180 degrees. 

 


 

   

   

 An analysis: Why We Use Fear for Entertainment 

 Why do many of us humans get so turned on by the psychological thriller sub-genre, horror movies, when fear in itself, is generally deemed a negative emotion?  There are some of us who actually go out looking for this kind of turn-on, a little at a time perhaps, but find it oddly to be an enjoyable endeavor.  We research it, pay for it, even stand in line to be able to get it first.   

 The end result is that we get scared, play havoc with our natural human systems, such as cause our hearts to race, our blood pressure to rise, sit on the edges of our seats with sweaty palms and bitten down nails and if it does all those things, reeks havoc like no other movie has done it before, we say we had a great time and at the same time, it was the scariest flick I have ever seen.  Next time we want to do this again, in order for the next one to top this one in quality, it has to wreak even more havoc with our senses. 

 Fear is a valuable emotion.  Our very existence, having survived human evolution to get to this place at the top of the food chain, is owed in a major way to fear and management thereof.  We have nothing to fear from fear itself.    

 Fear is the result of our brain's evolution, the mechanism that emerged to protect our ancestors from jumping from the tops of trees and going splat on the forest floor or following our sexual desires to the place where we find ourselves mating with giant grizzly bears, or at least, trying.   

 We are here because of fear.  It protects us and even though in reality, it is not known to be a pleasant emotion, it keeps our hearts beating and ensures that we will beget millions more beating human hearts in future human generations.  That being stated, inside of a darkened theater or a scary thrill ride in Las Vegas, manipulating our fears can be a whole lot of fun and always, always therapeutic. 

 Marina Warner in her book No Go the Bogeyman, states, "Fear has probably always played some part in amusement, but its peculiar pleasures have increasingly become an end in themselves ... the imagination often stirs up dread on purpose for its own sake."  

 Ever wonder why?  Let's explore the psychological thriller/horror film.  I see this genre as the film genre that has the job description of needing to be successfully hitting its mark by jarring the simple human emotion, fear.  In this genre is a grouping of stories having very similar patterns, which include even more similar characters.  It's as old as the proverbial hills, and as primal, and involves an extremely scary and out of control force the main characters have to deal with.  Scott and Susan, in my film Geist Haus are the main characters and they have to deal with what starts out almost innocently and ends up being a very scary entity. 

  In 1954, I was in my first movie theater experience.  I was 7 years old.  I was watching a 72 minute electronic animation directed by Michael Myerberg and John Paul, a version of the Humperdinck opera, Hansel and Gretel adapted for children, using spoken dialogue as well as Humperdinck's music.   To that point in my young life, it was the scariest thing I ever remember seeing in my 7 years of life and it sent me screaming into the lobby of the theater making my family oh-so proud.  That was about 57 years ago. 

 Hansel and Gretel is basically about a young sister and brother who are threatened by a cannibalistic witch who lives deep in the forest in a very attractive gingerbread house; children's fare. 

 It was many years later that it was pointed out to me that Aliens and Jaws were essentially the same movie as the movie that scared me so long ago.  Jaws is the story of a shark out to eat swimmers while Alien is about a monster that wants to do away with everyone on the ship.  These three movies have a huge entity that wants to devour the cast in a community from which the potential victims cannot escape. Pretty scary.   

 Another element is more psycho/spiritual.  Someone in every psychological/horror thriller has committed a sin, which supports belief systems like, be sure your sins will find you out, and what goes around, comes around and as ye sow, so shall ye also reap.  In Jaws, the mayor's greed made him block the news of a dangerous shark from getting out and the same sin is in Alien because the spaceship is a merchant vessel that decides not to return because it would be too costly, a serious financial loss, regardless of the danger imposed on the innocent; a sin.  Hansel and Gretel went against their parents' wishes by going deep into the forest which they had been told was forbidden. 

 I think this is a strong, almost undetectable ingredient that makes a film like this work.  It adds the element of guilt.  Guilt in this exercise as in many human interactions just spices things up by adding a naughty element to the plot, no matter how subtle. 

 This type of psychological thriller can only be called what it is, when it is done right, a scary movie.  The scarier and more horrifying, the more we enjoy it. 

 Reducing something to a formula is in no way accusing fellow film makers of ripping each other off.  Quite the contrary.  We have been borrowing this formula from others going back to the dragon-slayer stories told over and over for centuries as long ago as the Middle Ages.  It is a tried and true formula that works precisely for humans only because of our particular psychological make-up.  There is a horrible monster somewhere in the house or in our closed community and it is insane, completely unpredictable or there is a haunting, or my favorite, the complete unknown happening. 

 So, in order to achieve the psychological goal of the genre---fear, for entertainment purposes---we need three main ingredients, a scary entity, an enclosed place and sin. 

 Let me pick these apart a little.   

 When it comes to the entity, it must be a very powerful or supernatural entity.  The shark in Jaws was not just any shark.  It was a phenomenally huge and dangerous supercharged beast.  The beast was not only hungry.  It did not just want to eat.  It wanted to destroy, almost with a vengeance, so it has to be what all great scary entities have to be, insane, evil.  In Jaws, this realization was confirmed by the famous line, "We're going to need a bigger boat." 

 The scariest film I have seen as an adult is still, The Exorcist.  It was a jolting spiritual experience for me as a young man in my twenties because the underlying threat was not only shared fear for the life of the girl but the loss of her very soul.  I, at that time, had not settled on a comfortable spiritual belief for myself so I believe The Exorcist would have been comical if I did not believe that I actually had a soul.  But I did and therefore there was the almost subconscious realization that all of our souls were in danger, maybe even by viewing this flick.   

 Supernatural is best described, I think, as forces that are beyond our three dimensional understanding of reality.  The only thing I am sure of about the psychology of all humans is that no one knows for sure what is going on in the spiritual world, certainly beyond our three dimensional understanding of reality.  For this kind of filmmaking this knowledge creates great fodder to explore and push buttons that trigger fear.   

 Usually if a psychological thriller/horror movie is not satisfying---and the good ones are---it is because the elements just aren't there to make it prey on our most primal fears enough to jilt us into reacting. 

 What about the enclosed space?  Look at the boat in Jaws.  Look at the spaceship in Alien.  Look at the house, along with the responsibility of the babysitter to stay put and protect the children in 1979's "When A Stranger Calls."  Why is this structure so important?   

 As far back as the classic storytelling, the myth of the Minotaur, the thought of facing a half-man/half-bull was frightening to most people as it would be today.  However, it is even more psychologically jolting to face the same supernatural monster inside a darkened maze where there is no escape.  It ramps up the fear quotient substantially.  Hence, the more cramped our heroes, little or no way to escape, the better and more enjoyable our experience. 

 Now, the sin.  In the movie Scream, a character states this up front, "Have sex and die."  Guilt and sin are important to this genre.  In the more teen-oriented films, sex works best because it is not something around which teens have mastered their emotions.  What we haven't mastered, is scary.  The mothers in both The Ring and The Exorcist play from guilt they felt about always leaving their children with someone else while they tended to their high-powered careers.  These guilt buttons we can all identify with so are pretty much universal. 

 There must be a sin in a form that we all know, consciously or subconsciously, because our enjoyment is enhanced the easier it is to put ourselves in the role of the main character.  That it is the thing that makes it possible for the big scary entity to use our brain as its playground.  The universal identity is always there. 

  There is a sidebar sort of character that shows up in every film of this genre and he or she is damaged, usually by previous experience with the same or similar entity   In Jaws, he old sailor, Quint, with his scars, missing fingers and lore of past encounters and the old Priest we first meet in The Exorcist or Ash, half man, half robot in Alien.   

 We and the main character usually learn what a monstrosity we are up against here from this character and I have noticed also that more often than not, this character usually disappears a little over halfway into the film and is no longer there to offer their expertise which heightens the feeling of "all is lost" at the proper place in the film and puts us on the very edge of our psychic seats.   

 Just this week (12//2010) on CNN.com, I read an account of a woman. 44 years old who scores normally on IQ test, speaks well and has good memory faculties, but presents unusual brain structure damage resulting in her being, incapable of feeling fear.  It goes on to describe her delight in playing with dangerous, poisonous spiders and snakes, and partaking in all kinds of experiences that make people not afflicted so, shriek in terror.   

 She appeared to have a condition they thought to be genetic condition that had disabled, in both hemispheres, a region of the brain known as the amygdalae.  The Amygdalae is involved in processing emotional fears as well as memory.  For more than 20 years this woman had been studied y neurologists and to date, she has been unable to assess social situations that involve fear.  She is also not able to recognize evidence of fear on the faces of others. 

 


 

   

   

 Fear and The Excitation Factor 

 I thoroughly enjoy a good horror movie/psychological thriller.  I know firsthand that fear for me as well as others can be very exciting, even said to be enjoyable.  It is no secret that there is a certain awakening or arousal involved in the horror movie viewing process.  The historic fight or flight syndrome itself, many people find exciting.  Does this mean people enjoy being afraid?  Yes.  Some have even related the experience to sexual stimulation.  What's not to like about that? 

 Manufactured fear is considered for many humans, to be a rush.  New in Las Vegas, is X-Scream, a very scary amusement park sort of ride.  You take the basic childhood see-saw, put it almost 1000 feet above the ground and add a little bit of roller coaster to the mix.  In this case, the roller coaster track is only 69 feet long and does not take you back to safe terra firma as most coasters do.  This giant fear-button-pusher stops in midair at a 30-degree angle and riders are made to feel that they are going to fly into the ground almost hundreds of feet below. 

 Just when you think you're going to fall face first to your death, the track teeters backward.  The vehicle then slides back and forth across the pivoting track, reminiscent of a see saw, heightening the unsurety of the circumstance.  The entertainment value found in X Scream is excellently playing on the passenger's primal fears of falling (and from great, devastating heights, by literally launching one off the top of a building with no reasonable clue as to how you are going to get out of this one.  I have included a link to a Youtube® video of this ride.  If you are reading this on a computer that is also online, just point your browser to 

 http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JSOKHvc9Obw&feature=related 

 It is a fact that we humans are attracted to fear for entertainment purposes.  In his quest to answer as to why this appears to be so,  Dr. Arthur Aron's fear of heights research, had a group of male subjects walk across a 450 foot span rickety feeling bridge which was well over 200 feet in the air.  In the same study he had another grouping of male subjects walk across a very solidly built bridge, again well over 200 feet in the air.   

 Dr. Aron is purported to have had a very attractive female assistant whom he placed at the end of the bridge.  She conducted an interview with each of the participants asking them questions that were not related to the study whatsoever at the end of which she gave them each her phone number telling them that they can use it if they have any more questions. 

 There were 33 men in all who participated in the "stable bridge" experience.  Dr. Aron reported that of those 33, only two men made a follow-up call to the assistant while, the same number of men were used in the "unstable bridge" experiment and of those, nine men called the assistant.  Dr. Aron concluded from this that sexual attraction is stimulated by fear.   Many of us thoroughly enjoy the process of sexual attraction and if sexual attraction is indeed stimulated by fear, some of us may find this process very entertaining. 

 Fear also serves a role in self-preservation.  If it was that we could be fearless, it is arguable whether we would have survived very long as a species.  We most likely would have been done away with by us walking into lion's dens, volcanoes and the like.  It appears that it is fear, placed appropriately that guarantees the passing on of our genes. 

 In the evolutionary discussion of the eighteen hundreds, something called the "face of fear" became a topic; that image of the human face, not unlike the famous, 'The Scream' painting by Edvard Munch which now hangs in the Munch Museum in Oslo, Norway, of the same period, the face of sheer horror.   

 It seems like virtually every human, regardless of background, makes that expression when terrified.  Many believed that this was a gift from the spirits or genes which enables us to instantaneously, without thinking, alert others of danger, even those who do not speak the same language as us.   

 Nineteenth century Charles Robert Darwin thought more pragmatically, that this face was merely  

 our instinct causing the muscles to tighten.  Interestingly, in the same study, he reported, "I put  

 my face close to the thick glass-plate in front of a puff-adder in the Zoological Gardens, with the 

  firm determination of not starting back if the snake struck at me; but, as soon as the blow was  

 struck, my resolution went for nothing, and I jumped a yard or two backwards with astonishing  

 rapidity. My will and reason were powerless against the imagination of a danger which had never  

 been experienced.."  (Darwin, 1872, p. 105) 

 In our everyday modern world, we have little to fear from the wild but the instinct still serves us well while maneuvering around our cities, ever aware of the dangers that lurk and threaten our money, autos, safety and even our lives. 

 When I am traveling from the facility I work in to home, at night, I am careful to travel a different route than I would during the day, because I fear that breaking down in a higher crime area at night would challenge my safety more than if I take another route through a safer, maybe more well-lighted neighborhood even though it may add mileage and time to my trip.  This is from a planned, rational fear designed to ensure my survival in case of trouble.  The stimulus has been replaced, to be sure.  However where I live--now the most dangerous city in America--it could be argued that we are in as much danger as we ever were. 

 It's in the genes.  I have never had a poisonous snake attack me but if I did, chances are that I would react exactly like Darwin had, so this reaction is not experience-based.  That's why, in my film, I could use the same tried and true set-up of making the audience think someone is behind the door, the door opens, nobody is there, then close the door, and there is the monster accompanied by a loud, calamitous sound and the audience shrieks.  I have played on their hereditary as well as their experienced based fears, have them survive and then add the element of surprise and in this instant, audience members shriek, grab their partners, hug real tight and then laugh.   

 I have gone to hundreds of these screenings and noticed that also, many people grab their partners, hide their eyes, peak through their fingers, shriek, laugh and then kiss and then nuzzle closer, as if nuzzling will protect us from the all-powerful invading monsters.  It is almost as if we are designing a mood.  Like designing a romantic setting, we are designing a fearful setting so we can comfort and feel comforted. 

 When we see a rat, which is a much smaller creature than humans and by itself not a formidable opponent, most of us cringe and go into fear mode.  I believe that it just may be that it is in our genes that the image of the rat triggers memories of a large portion of our species being nearly wiped off the planet by the plague in the fifteenth century.  In a case like this, it could be that we have genetically inbred self-preserving fear. 

 One of the attributes of fear we storytellers utilize is anticipatory fear.  We, as well as other creatures have the ability to suggest horror that may or may not occur.  Most of us have never been in a horrible car accident but as the speed increases or the driving becomes more risky, we anticipate the terror we would experience if a terrible accident were to happen.  We have the ability to stimulate our fear and provoke a similar response in ourselves if it were to actually happen.   

 This is similar to Pavlovian thinking on involuntary reflex actions which taught me techniques like, when I am thirsty and I can't get to any water, all I have to do is think of myself biting into halves of pulpy, juicy lemons and taking mouthfuls of the contents and chewing them up.  This always results in my salivary glands overflowing---as yours are right now, if you followed the thought pattern I just described---and it delays my thirst for a time.    

 


 

   

   

 Then there is Conditioning. 

 The human fear system seems to be two fold; response as well as conditioning.  Conditioning accounts for why some people express fear of Chihuahuas as if they were vampires from hell while the rest of us find them cuddly and cute.  Well, maybe not Chihuahuas.   

 In the beginning of the twentieth century, one John Watson, who was a psychologist, actually conditioned an infant who previously expressed no fear of animals, to have a fear of white laboratory rats.  Watson did so by utilizing good old Pavlovian conditioning by introducing the neutral stimulus with the negative effect. (Dubuc, 2002) 

 I wonder if John Watson the psychologist was proud of himself when he achieved the result of every time the infant reached out to a pet rat, when his team of assistants would create horrifying loud noises behind the infant.  The baby soon learned to fear little creatures.   

 This, as well as a human fearing Chihuahuas is the result of conditioned response.   Most likely, the person who fears Chihuahuas had some kind of a bad experience or multiple experiences with a Chihuahua or other small animal resulting in the site of Chihuahuas becoming a trigger.   

   

   

 Phobias 

 According to Dr. Edward J. Bourne, who has specialized in the treatment of anxiety disorders and related problems for two decades, a phobia is an irrational, intense and persistent fear of certain situations, activities, things, animals, or people.  The main symptom of this disorder is the excessive and unreasonable desire to avoid the feared stimulus.  When the fear is beyond one's control, and if the fear is interfering with daily life, then a diagnosis under one of the anxiety disorders can be made (Gersley, 2001). 

 I have learned that phobias come in three principle forms.   

 There is agoraphobia which is a disorder that may develop from the fear of having a panic attack in an environment where there is no reasonable means of escaping to safety.   

 Then there is social phobia which encompasses fears having to do with social situations and other people and can include eating in public places, performance anxiety, in general fears associated with embarrassment by scrutiny of others. 

 Then there are specific phobias which are fears associated with a particular situation or even a particular thing which often include fear of the site of blood, fear of heights, public speaking and many other specific issues.   

 A 2005 Gallup Polling resulted in the following common fears of teens in America.  Among the top ten were terrorist attacks, spiders, failure, death, heights and being alone.  Many adults fear the same things, which begs the question, are there such things as universal fears?  (Bourke, 2006). 

 I found that some research demonstrates that we all may be genetically wired for fear of certain common harmful things like other creatures that once were considered challenging because of their poison or propensity to carry disease which challenged our survival.  As in Darwin's experiment, having never been in the presence of a snake, many humans recoil at the sight of one, which is a mystery if one does not accept the theory that evolutionary has imbedded fear, instinctually into us.   

 I remember the television show "Fear Factor" which offered money to contestants if they would eat raw fish milkshakes, worms, live roaches etc. and how it may have been more interesting, as well as entertaining, if Dr. Martin Seligman had directed  it.  Seligman conducted an experiment in conditioning by utilizing human subjects for which he displayed photographs of particular articles.  After showing the photos, he shocked them with electricity.  He was attempting to actually create phobias in them or what he called an intense, irrational fear of whatever was displayed in the photograph (Cowley, 2003). 

 Photographs of vermin common to us all (snakes and spiders) required a minimal two to four shocks.  On the other hand, when the photograph was of something commonly held as pretty or beautiful like a tree or a beautiful flower, it took many more shocks to create a similar phobic response.  These results appeared to give credence to the idea of a universal fear theory.  

 There are also more individualized fears, city fears, cultural fears and regional fears.  I grew up in the tenements of New York so I may have a more finely honed, experience-based fear of being robbed than rural-based humans.  People here in Flint, MI have little fear or even concern with hurricanes.  I do because I lived on and sailed in the Gulf of Mexico for over thirty years and I have experienced their destructive capabilities.  We appear to fear some things instinctively while other fears appear to stem from experiences we have lived through.   

 A curious example; according to the International Journal of Psychiatry in Clinical Practice, in Japan there is a culturally specific phobia named Taijin Kyofusho described  herein as "the fear of offending other persons by an excess of modesty or showing respect." 

 Fear is normal and even can be defined as good when it aids in self-preservation.  It becomes debilitating when fears interfere with healthful living, making our lives unmanageable and resulting in disorders.   

 Nineteen million people in the America suffer from mental disorders that involve irrational fears according to NIMH and these include Stress, Post Traumatic Stress, Anxiety and Panic disorders. (National Institute Of Mental Health, 2010).  

 For my purpose though, it is of utmost importance to understand the process of how we humans are able to use our fears for entertainment and yes, with some therapeutic purpose. 

 In our human brains, the amygdalae perform primary roles in the storing process and formation of memories which are associated with events of an emotional nature.  During fear conditioning, stimuli come directly from our senses and arrive at the basolateral complexes of the amygdalae.  Human association between stimuli and our prediction of aversive events is believed to be mediated by what is termed long-term potentiation.  Long-term potentiation can be seen as a sustained enhancement of signaling between affected neurons. 

 It of course gets a little more involved than this but it is important to comprehend that any damage to the amygdalae impairs both the expression of Pavlovian fear conditioning and even the acquisition of this conditioning.  So much so that rats with damaged amygdalae have been known to walk right up to very large, snarling, dangerous cats; usually their arch-enemies.  

 Barely into the twenty-first century, Los Angeles psychiatrist Dr. Mark Barad of UCLA did an experiment in which was combined a noise with an electrical shock.  A tone was played to rats followed by an immediate electrical shock to the metal floor of the rats' cage; classical conditioning.  It didn't take long before the rats started bracing themselves immediately after the tone was initiated.  They were bracing themselves in response to the learned expectation of a shock following the tone.  Their amygdalae had associated the shock with the sound.  The sound without the shock eventually elicited a fear response. (Layton, 2005)   

 Dr. Barad then began the process of fear-extinction in which he made the sound but no longer applied the shock.  As expected, after a number of times hearing the sound without the shock, the rats stopped bracing themselves for the shock.  Transferring this approach to humans is found in most behavioral therapies for fear extinction focus because we too, focus on exposure.   

 A fear of reptiles may involve baby steps of finding a zoo and visiting the reptile exhibit several times and then later, making additional visits, each time ramping up a closer involvement with the creature under very controlled circumstances until the subject can actually experience coming into contact with it.  This process increases the probability that, after several times, new extinction memories will be formed and they may deliver the message that under controlled circumstances, the contact with the reptiles are not to be feared.  This serves to contradict the original fear that coming in any new contact with a reptile under any circumstances perhaps is to be feared and avoided.   

 This treatment can add to the manageability and the quality of one's life. 

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

   

 Pharmaceutical Solutions 

 There is a hindering protein N-methyl D-asparate (NMDA) in the amygdalae that doesn't necessarily but certainly inhibits fear extinction.  Therefore the reasoning developed that possibly stimulating that protein might also help actuate the process of fear extinction.  For example D-cycloserine, an antibiotic used in the treatment of tuberculosis also may useful in the treatment of fear extinction by assisting the action of NMDA in the amygdalae in combination--as well as to act as a possible accelerator of the process--with other behavioral oriented therapies that attempt to create fear-extinction memories. 

 This opinion was illustrated in a study done with rats.  The rats had been previously conditioned this time to associate a bright light flash with electrical shock to the feet.  When the light flashed repeatedly without being followed by the shock, rats injected with D-cycloserine appeared to have their fear-extinction process accelerated beyond those that were not injected.  Similar results were realized when D-cycloserine was used in a study with humans who presented with fear of heights. 

 In this study, after the subjects were exposed to virtual-reality-heights---for safety reasons---those who had exposed themselves to heights in reality reported they were able to do it twice as often as individuals who had not been treated with the pharmaceutical therapy.   

 I think the results of D-cycloserine studies offer hope to individuals afflicted with debilitating phobias/disorders. 

 


 

   

 This is your brain on fear. 

 As we have established, fear originates in parts of the brain called the amygdalae, a set of neurons in close proximity to each other involved in the processing of emotions.  Without the amygdalae, we would lack the capacity to feel fear. 

 Pertinent to this creative project, whether a ferocious bear lunges toward us in real life or from a movie screen, our brain's initial response will be essentially the same. 

 Here's the kicker for me and my purposes.  Even if we're in a safe context, like the movie theater, the otherwise harmful stimulus triggers our amygdalae and this process ends up releasing hormones in our brains as well as our bodies.  However, simultaneously our prefrontal cortex reasons and interprets this incoming information at the same time reminding us that it's only a film and therefore not a real threat.  We are able to delude our minds and our bodies into having real physiological reactions. 

 The pleasurable fear, as I call it, during the process of viewing scary movies is derived from an intricate dance that occurs between the primitive part of our brain (amygdalae) and the logical part (prefrontal cortex).  As this amygdalae/prefrontal cortex dance occurs, we are simply entertained by the resulting attractive sensations.  It appears that the very incongruity of these two entities interacting, for a period of time, is read by the rest of our brain and bodies as just plain fun. 

 


 

   

 Context matters 

 I have found that when I see something scary, my body responds the same whether it is real or not.  Just like when it is real, my brain discovers a change in environment as if I changed surroundings and circumstances and it follows it appears to follow its learned behavior of automatically releasing dopamine to keep me on my toes, make me more alert.  I believe that with horror flicks, when anything jumps out at me from the screen, my brain starts pumping out dopamine.  So realistically, when I am writing a screenplay, I am dipping into responses and manipulating them; responses that probably took thousands of years to develop according to Professor of science at NYU, Joseph LeDoux. (Iaccino, 1994) 

 As to why some people like to be frightened more than others, it appears to be therapeutic to some of us that watching scary movies is a way of overcoming fears and limitations.  This could be the reason why frightening video games as well as movies appear to be more popular with children than with adults.  They are at an age where they are just pushing their boundaries to just see what happens.  

 Adult entertainment has many similarities.  Some adult human beings appear to have a lifelong affair with process that involves testing of the boundaries.  Although, for me, exploring this area brings immediately to mind the scary-movie-amygdalae-prefrontal-cortex-syndrome, because the feeling that is derived from this entertaining dance is so similar.   

 Entering into extreme sexual situations that one is pre-programmed to have fear of, appears to trigger the amygdalae and prefrontal cortex into a very similar dance, although I have found that what causes a grown adult to test boundaries or seek thrills is difficult to answer with any specifics. It may go back to the nurture vs. Nature discussion because each of us, as adults is an amalgam of a very complex combination of both genetic and environmental influences.   

 On a level of lesser taboo, some sensation junkies appear to be more receptive to dopamine excitation as with extreme sporting activities.  Some studies appear to suggest sensation seeking aficionados of this ilk seem more receptive to dopamine arousal.  In the case of extreme sports devotees, the process of seeking the reward and reinforcement of the release of chemicals in the brain is many times likened to all symptoms pointing to their having just received pharmacological intervention, which indeed they have. 

 I think in a sense that we humans may be one of the most fearful species because I believe we have a propensity to worry more than I am aware other species do.   

 According to reasoning collected so far, it appears that we experience two kinds of fear. There is fear resulting from evolutionary formed genetic make-up which causes us to uncontrollably fear things that our ancestors feared, which endured their survival as well as our very existence. 

 Then there are our acquired, experience based fears with which some association with suffering is made in our brains and is sometimes triggered. 

 If, for instance, as a child, I was beaten up by kids bigger than me, I may have fear triggered by merely being around kids bigger than me.  If I swim in the Gulf of Mexico and get stung by a Sting Rey, I may prefer pools and feel uncomfortable on the beach even looking out at the ocean.  This association can go away as experiences prove that these associations are not necessary every time one is exposed to the stimulus but gone unchecked, these fears can become irrational to the place that it interferes with the manageability of one's life.   

 It appears to be that our rationality informs us that it is ridiculous to be afraid of certain assumptions that we turn into fear-based worry while simultaneously, primal, inherited fears just will not let go of.   

 I have in my scary book collection, Stephen King's "Night Shift", a gathering of stories from undoubtedly the most successful marketer in the exploitation of our amygdalae/prefrontal cortex dance for fun and profit.  In the forward of this collection, King described the dance so many of us enjoy this way.  "I don't like to sleep with one leg sticking out.  Because if a cool hand ever reached out from under the bed and grasped my ankle, I might scream.  That sort of thing doesn't happen, of course, and we all know that.  The thing under my bed waiting to grab my ankle isn't real.  I know that, and I also know that if I'm careful to keep my foot under the covers, it will never be able to grab my ankle."  (King, Forward, 1979). 

 This of course makes no sense whatsoever and at the same time is fun.  Like many therapeutic approaches to many psychological challenges, they aren't real, may metaphorically represent reality, but are oh so useful for our unique human purposes. 

 Once again, we have nothing to fear from fear itself. We find it, at times, quite fun.
