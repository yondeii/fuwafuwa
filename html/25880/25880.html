はじめに

 

　この本は主に英語の速読に初めて挑戦される方のために書きました。日本語の速読もできないのに英語の速読などとてもできないと思われる方も多いのではないかと思いますが、あきらめてはいけません。この本の中でご紹介するテクニックをマスターしていただければみなさまの読解スピードは大幅に上がります。今後も企業のグローバル化が進み、英語を読む機会や必要性が増えてくることは確実です。しかも、ゆっくりと時間をかけて読むだけではすまされなくなってきました。早く正確に読んでその結果を仕事やテストに反映させることが求められます。TOEIC、TOEFL、英検などを受験される皆さまは、特に問題に時間をかけて読んでいたのでは、すべての問題を解くことができなくなってしまいます。しかも、完全に読まないで答えて正解できるほど問題はやさしく はありません。

　この本の中でご紹介するいろいろなテクニックを素直に実践してみてください。過去に実際に効果のあったテクニックだけを取り上げてあります。速読のテクニックを学ばずに、ただ自己流でがんばっただけでは限界があります。

　この本の中には、私がこの４０年間にわたって、欧米の色々な速読の本や速読コースから身につけた基礎をご紹介しております。

第１ 日目　自分の読解速度を知る

 

最初に現在の英文の読解速度を知ってもらいます。現在の速度を知った上で、その読解速度をスピードアップするお手伝いをさせていただきます。まず短いエッセイを読んでもらいます。このエッセイは、ほぼ６００語で書かれています。６分で読み終えることができれば、６００割る６で、あなたの1分間の英文読解速度は100語ということになります。もし、１２分かかったとすると、1分間に50語となります。読むのが遅いということは、何か原因があります。その主な原因として考えられるのは一般的に次の5種類です。

 

1.        喉や唇を動かしている

2.       小声を出して発音している

3.       返り読みをしている

4.      一語一語読んでいる

5.       ボキャブラリーが不足している

 

　これらの原因をひとつずつ解消していけば、読解速度は着実に速くなっていきます。現在の速度が、遅くても気にすることは全くありません。遅ければ遅いほど、速く読めるようになったときの達成感や喜びは大きくなるからです。私も、速読を勉強し始めた20代の前半には、英文を速く読めませんでした。このitは何を意味しているのだろうか、このthatは何のことだろうかと、一語一語を正確に理解してから読んでいたからでした。この一語一語読む癖は、学校の英語読解の授業でついたものだと思っています。この癖も含め、読解速度を遅くしているすべての癖を解消できる方法を2日目以降にご紹介して行きます。

ちなみに私が教えてい た国立大学の大学院の生徒が１分間に読む速度を調査してみました。すると、中国人大学院生およびその他のアジアの国の大学院生たちの英文読解速度は、１分間に１５５語でした、一方日本人の大学院生は、１分間に１０６語でした。読んだ後に１０問の質問に答えてもらったスコアは、中国人学生たちが８７点で、日本人学生たちは８３点でした。

American History Class  --１ -- Christopher Columbus

 

"Today," says Mr. Sears, "we will talk about Christopher Columbus. Christopher Columbus is a very important person in American history. He was a great navigator. A navigator is someone who explores by ship. Does anyone know what Christopher Columbus discovered?"

"He discovered America," says Peter.

"That's right," says Mr. Sears. "Columbus discovered not only America, but also many other important places. He is credited with discovering North America, South America and many important islands such as Cuba and the Bahamas. Does anyone know where Columbus was born?"

"He was born in Italy," says Tony.

"Correct," says Mr. Sears. "Christopher Columbus was born in the town of Genoa, Italy in 1451. He was Italian, but for most of his life he lived and worked in Spain. Our story begins in 1492. At this time, Spain was ruled by a very famous king and queen. Does anyone know their names?"

"In 1492," says Samantha, "Spain was ruled by King Ferdinand and his wife Queen Isabella."

"Very good," says Mr. Sears. "King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella wanted to trade goods with the people in Asia. In the past, Europeans had traded with Asia by traveling east. This took a very long time. Christopher Columbus believed that he could reach Asia in a much shorter time by traveling west. The King and Queen gave him permission to go search for Asia. He left Spain in late 1492. With him he took three ships and ninety seamen. Seamen are people that work on a ship at sea. Columbus' three ships are very famous. Does anyone know what these three ships were named?"

"The Nina, the Pinta, and the Santa Maria," says Josh.

"Yes," says Mr. Sears, "Christopher Columbus' ships were named the Nina, the Pinta, and the Santa Maria. These are Spanish names. When he left Spain, Columbus was sure that he could sail directly to Asia. Do you think that it is possible to sail directly from Spain to Asia?"

"No," says Tom. "It is not possible."

"Right," says Mr. Sears. "Today, we know that the continents of North America and South America lie between Europe and Asia. There are also many islands between them. In 1492, however, the Europeans did not know exactly how big the world was. They did not know that North America, South America, or the surrounding islands existed. When Columbus reached Cuba, he thought that he had reached Japan. When he saw South America, he thought that he was looking at mainland China."

"So," exclaims Peter, "America was discovered by accident!"

"That's true," says Mr. Sears. "In 1493, Columbus returned to Spain. He told King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella what he had found. The King and Queen were very happy. They gave him a great reward. They also gave him permission to sail west three more times. During these trips he explored many important places in and around the Americas."

"Did Columbus meet the Native Americans?" asks Josh.

"Good question," says Mr. Sears. "The lands that Columbus explored were not empty. When Columbus reached the Americas he found that people were already living there.  Columbus called them Indians. Now, we call them Native Americans. Many different groups of Native Americans lived in the Americas and on the nearby islands. They had lived there for many thousands of years before Columbus came."

The bell rings.

第２日目　小声を出す癖を直す

 

自分では気づかないことが多いのですが、英語を読むときに小声を出している人がたくさんいます。この人たちの発音する速度と読み速度は同じということになります。なぜなら、どんなに早く発音しても、その読もうとする努力が障害になって、それ以上の早さで読むことはできないからです。普通の大きさの声を出しても、小声で発音しても、ほとんど聞こえないようなかすかな声で発音しても、結局は同じことです。読むときに、発音しているかどうかは、自分の喉に指を当てればわかります。喉は使わずに、唇だけ動かしている人もいます。それは、人差し指を唇にあててみればわかります。これらの癖を直すのは比較的簡単です。喉を震わせていることがわかったならば、人差し指を中指の腹を喉に強く押し付け、その状態で発音してみてください。唇を震わせる癖のある人は、シィーと相手を黙らせるときのように人差し指を強く唇に押し付けてください。こうすれば、唇を動かすことができなくなるはずです。

これら三つの癖を一緒に直す方法もあります。それは鉛筆を口ではさむか、チューインガムを多めに口に入れることです。こうすれば読みながら発音するのが難しくなるからです。友人や家族の人に、自分が、読むときに声を出していないか、喉やくちびるを震わせていないかをチェックしてもらうのもよいでしょう。

次のエッセイを1回目は、何もしないでいつものやり方で読み時間を計ってください。2回目は、声を出さないように心がけ、指を使って喉や唇を押さえて読み、時間を計ってく記録してください。1度目よりも、2度目のほうが読む速度が上がるのを体感できるはずです。それぞれの読むのにかかった時間を記録してください。

American History Class -- 2 -- Native Americans

 

"Last time," begins Mr. Sears, "we discussed Christopher Columbus. We learned that when Christopher Columbus reached the Americas, he found a group of people already living there. He called them Indians. Today, we call them Native Americans. Native American culture is very interesting."

"Where are the Native Americans now?" asks Frank.

"Today, there are more than three million Native Americans living in the United States and Canada," says Mr. Sears. 

"How many Native Americans lived here when Columbus came?" asks Cindy.

"When the first Europeans came to North America," says Mr. Sears, "there were more than  sixteen million Native Americans living here."

"Did they all live together?" asks Frank.

"The Native Americans were divided into many groups," says Mr. Sears. "These groups were called tribes. Each tribe had its own name, language, and leaders. Do you know the names of any of the Native American tribes?"

"Cherokee," say several of the students.

"Mohawk," says Josh.

"Navajo," says Peter.

"Good," says Mr. Sears. "The Cherokee, Mohawk, and Navajo were all names of Native American tribes. There were also many other tribes. The Cherokee were one of the biggest Native American tribes. They lived in what is now the southeastern part of the United States, particularly in the states of Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Virginia, and Kentucky."

"Were all the different tribes friends?" asks Frank.

"Well," says Mr. Sears, "some tribes were friends. Other tribes fought with their neighbors. Tribes would often trade together."

"What did the Native Americans eat?" asks Cindy.

"They hunted and fished," says Mr. Sears. "They ate many kinds of animals and fish. Some favorites were deer, bears, and small animals like rabbits and squirrels. Some tribes also grew squash, rice, and corn. When they killed an animal, they didn't waste anything. They ate the meat, made clothes from the skin and fur, and used the bones to make tools."

"How did they hunt?" asks Cindy.

"The hunters used a bow," says Josh. "Their arrows were tipped with arrowheads."

"What's an arrowhead?" asks Cindy.

"Good question," says Mr. Sears. "The arrowhead is the sharp point of a Native American arrow. The Native Americans made arrowheads from a special kind of stone called flint. Flint is a very hard stone and can be made very sharp."

"My father found some arrowheads," says David. "He found them when he was digging in the garden."  

"Good," says Mr. Sears. "Old arrowheads are often found in America and Canada. For example, in a small American town called Williamsburg, Kentucky a lot of Native American arrowheads were unearthed. Many people collect arrowheads. They are an interesting piece of history."

"What did their homes look like," asks Tony.

"They lived in many different kinds of homes," says Mr. Sears. "Most of their homes were made of dried mud and bark. Some Native Americans lived in large round tents. These were called tepees. Often many relatives would live together in the same home."

"What was the religion of the Native Americans?" asks David.

"The Native Americans believed in many different gods and spirits," says Mr. Sears. "Each tribe had their own stories and legends. Native American legends are very fascinating. These stories are full of spirits, gods, talking animals, and other interesting creatures."

The bell rings.

"Next time," says Mr. Sears, "we will talk about the colonists. The colonists were the first Europeans to settle in North America."

知らない単語や意味があいまいな単語やフレーズがでてくると、どうしても後戻りしてもう一度読みたくなるものです。しかしそのように返り読みをちょくちょくすることは速読の妨げになります。実際にはそのまま読み続けていれば、意味が分かることが多いものです。

この癖のある人は、絶対に後戻りして読み返さないという強い意志を持つことが大切です。しかし長い間についた癖は、そう簡単に直せるものではありません。そこで、カードを使ってこの癖を直すことをおすすめします。読もうとする本の１ページの横幅より５センチくらい長いカードか厚紙を用意します。縦は８から１０センチにします。たとえば１ページの幅が１０センチの横書きの英語の本を読むのであれば１５センチの幅で、縦は１０センチのカードを作ってください。横幅をページよりも長くするのは、手でつかむためのスペースが必要だからです。縦を１０センチ ほどにする理由は、すでに読んだ部分を大きく隠すためです。しおりをカード代わりに使うのはよくありません。しおりだと横幅も縦も十分ではなく、返り読みできてしまうからです。カードの色は、黒かグレーがよいでしょう。赤、黄、青や白のような明るい色は選ばないでください。何度も見ていると、目も疲れますし気も散るからです。このカードを右手か左手でつかんで、読んだ部分を上から隠し、これから読む行の上の真上に置きます。読み終わった行は確実に隠してください。また同じ行の中でわからなかった単語やフレーズの返り読みは絶対にしないでください。最初のうちは、とかく返り読みをしたくなるものですが、強い意志でがんばってください。カードを使って、数週間続ければ、必ず、返り読みの癖は解消することができるはずです。根気よくトライし続けてください。

人間は記憶するためには、目をいったん止める必要があります。従って、目を一旦停止せずにずっと動かし続けている読み方は、記憶するのには適していません。しかし、読んですぐに、目を移動しなければ速読はできません。それで、必要になってくるのがペーサーです。これは、ペースを作るものという意味です。読むための速度やペースを決める際に、目と自分の意志だけに頼っていては、なかなかうまくいかないものです。

いつでも使えるペーサーとして、ご自分の指を使う方法をご紹介します。まず、人差し指、中指、薬指の３本の指の間を１センチずつ開いて本の左端の上に乗せてください。そしてその３本の指の先端をそろえてください。中指を１センチほど下げるとよいでしょう。それから、その３本の指の間を保ったまま、右に約１センチずつ一旦停止しながら移動させてみてください。ページの最後まできたら、次の行に移ってください。そして、また同じように右に移動させてください。横に移動させるときに、中指は曲げたままにしておいてください。１センチ移動のペースができるようになったら、次は、２センチ間隔で移動させてみてください。それができたら、３センチ間隔もやってみてください。最初はスムースに移動しにくいとは思いますが、練習を積めばできるようになります。次に、その指の真上の文字を読んでみてください。３本指の上なので、大体３文字読むことになります。最初は、１センチ間隔で、次は２センチ間隔で、最後は３センチ間隔でやってみてください。今度は、真上の文字だけでなく、指の間にある文字も一緒に読むようにしてください。やはり１センチ間隔から、３センチ間隔まで３通りをやってみてください。最初はまとまった単語の意味を一瞬にして理解することは、難しいかもしれません。しかしすでに内容がわかっている単語のまとまりなので、理解できるようになるはずです。

American History Lesson -- 5 -- The Civil War

 

"Today," says Mr. Sears, "we will talk about the Civil War. As you know, America is made up of many states. In the Civil War, the Southern states fought against the Northern states. Does anyone know when the Civil War began?"

"1861," says Josh.

"Good," says Mr. Sears. "The Civil War began in 1861 when the Southern army attacked a Northern army base. This base was called Fort Sumter. Fort Sumter was located in the state of South Carolina. The war lasted for four years. Does anyone know why the North and the South were fighting?"

"They were fighting because the South had slaves," says Tony.

"That's right," says Mr. Sears. "In the Southern states there were many giant farms. These farms were called plantations. On plantations, all of the work was done by slaves. Slaves are people that are forced to work for free. Some people in the South owned more than five-hundred slaves."

"Where did the slaves come from," asks Ralph.

"The slaves were brought to America from Africa as prisoners," says Mr. Sears. "Then they were sold to the plantation owners in the South."

"That is very cruel," says David.

"Yes," says Mr. Sears. "The people in the North also thought it was very cruel to own slaves. They thought the people in the South should let their slaves be free. The President also thought the slaves should be free. Does anyone know who the President of the United States was at this time?"

"Abraham Lincoln," says Peter.

"Right," says Mr. Sears. "Abraham Lincoln was the sixteenth President of the United States. He decided to pass a new law that would make all of the slaves free. The people in the south didn't think this was fair because they had paid a lot of money for their slaves. This is why the North fought against the South. Eleven states fought for the South. Twenty-three states fought for the North. It was a very terrible war. Does anyone know why?"

"I think," says Peter, "that the Civil War was very terrible because it divided the country."

"That's right," says Mr. Sears. "The Civil War divided the country. It also divided many families. Often, brother would be forced to fight against brother. It was a very bad situation."

"How many people died in the war," asks Cindy.

"More Americans died in the Civil War than in any other war," says Mr. Sears. "Over one million people were killed or wounded. Can anyone name the most famous battle of the Civil War?"

"Gettysburg," says Tony.

"Good," says Mr. Sears, "The most famous battle of the Civil War was the Battle of Gettysburg. Gettysburg is in the state of Pennsylvania. This battle lasted for three days. Finally, the North won and the South was forced to retreat. The South lost many soldiers in this battle. There were a few smaller battles afterwards, but the South had lost all of its power at Gettysburg. Finally, in 1865, the South surrendered to the North. The slaves became free and were allowed to become American citizens." 

"I have been to the battlefield at Gettysburg," says Josh. "It was very interesting."

"That's a good point, Josh," says Mr. Sears. "Many of the battlefields of the Civil War are now popular tourist attractions in America. A battlefield is the place where a battle happened. Many people are very interested in the Civil War. They enjoy visiting the battlefields. Going to see a Civil War battlefield is like stepping back in time."

The bell rings.

"Next time," says Mr. Sears, "we will talk about the American Old West. It was a very exciting time in American history."

American History Lesson -- 6 -- The Old West

 

"Today," says Mr. Sears, "we will talk about the  Old West. It was a very exciting time in American history. As we know, the first Americans all lived in the eastern part of the United States. The United States wanted to become bigger. So, the government decided to give free land to anyone willing to move to the western part of the country. Many people moved west to claim land and start a new life."

"What did the people do when they came to the west?" asks Mary.

"Many of them used their new land to create ranches," says Mr. Sears. "A ranch is a large area of land where animals are raised. Most of the ranches in the Old West were cattle ranches. The people who work at ranches are called ranchers or cowboys. Cowboys worked very hard and they had a reputation as being very tough. They often carried guns to protect their cattle from bandits."

"What are bandits?" asks Josh.

"Bandits are groups of traveling thieves," says Mr. Sears. "Bandits were very common in the Old West. They would travel from place to place, stealing what they could from the people. They often tried to steal cattle from the ranchers. They would also rob trains and banks. Bandits became even more common after the Gold Rush."

"What was the Gold Rush?" asks Frank.

"In January of 1848," says Mr. Sears, "a carpenter named John Marshall was traveling alongside the American River in California. Suddenly, something shiny caught his eye. He stopped, reached into the clear water, and pulled out a large chunk of pure gold."

"Wow," exclaims Josh, "what a lucky guy!"

"Yes," says Mr. Sears, "he was very lucky. News of Marshall's big discovery traveled quickly. Within a year people from all over the country were moving west.  They all wanted to become rich by finding more gold. This period of American history is known as the Gold Rush."

"Did they all look for gold in the river?" asks Cindy.

"Actually," says Mr. Sears, "most gold is found underground. The people who dig for gold or other metals underground are called miners. Many miners would gather in places where there was a lot of gold to be found. They would build towns. These towns were called mining towns."

"What was like life in a mining town?" asks Cindy.

"Life in a mining town was both very exciting and dangerous," says Mr. Sears. "The gold attracted many bandits. Most of the people wore guns on their belts. People who carry guns to protect themselves are called gunslingers. Usually, if two gunslingers had a dispute they would settle it by having a shoot-out. Can anyone tell me what a shoot-out is?"              

"I know," says Tony. "A shoot-out is when two people meet in the middle of the street and shoot their guns at one another. Whoever lives is the winner!"

"That's right," says Mr. Sears.

"That sounds very violent," says Samantha.

"I have seen many shoot-outs in movies," says Tony. "I love westerns!" 

"Yes," says Mr. Sears. "Movies about the Old West are very popular in America. These movies are called  westerns.  Westerns often include gunfights between cowboys and bandits or shoot-outs between famous gunslingers. Westerns are very exciting!"

"What happened to all of the mining towns?" asks Josh.

"Well," says Mr. Sears, "eventually there was no more gold to be found. People left the mining towns and returned to the cities. Some of the old mining towns still exist today. We call them ghost towns because no one lives there anymore. The streets and buildings are all empty."

Christmas in America

 

It's Christmas Eve in America! Frank stands on his porch watching snow fall slowly from the night sky. It covers the ground in a thick white blanket. All around him, the neighborhood is filled with the sights and sounds of Christmas. 

The houses are covered with colorful Christmas lights. Christmas lights are very popular in America. The lights flash red, green, and blue. The lights reflect on the snow, making the whole neighborhood seem alive with color! 

On the street nearby, a group of men, women, and children are singing Christmas carols. Christmas carols are joyous songs that are only sung during the Christmas holiday. They sing carols to entertain their neighbors. The people smile when they hear these beautiful songs. Sometimes the neighbors invite the carolers inside for cookies and eggnog. Eggnog is traditional Christmas beverage.

"Frank," his mother calls from inside, "It's almost time for bed!"

Frank looks up at the night sky. He is expecting a special visitor. On Christmas Eve, Santa Claus travels from house to house leaving gifts for all the good boys and girls. Frank does not see Santa yet, but he is sure that Santa is on his way.

Inside, Frank hangs his stocking by the fireplace. A stocking is a large colorful sock. Tonight, Santa will fill his stocking with candy and small gifts. Frank also places a plate of cookies and a glass of milk near the Christmas tree. This is a snack for Santa.

Frank pauses to admire the Christmas tree. It is covered with flashing lights, silver tinsel, and shiny ornaments. At the top is a large golden star. Beneath the tree are gifts of all shapes and sizes. They are wrapped in colorful paper. 

Lying in bed, Frank thinks of all the wonderful gifts Santa will bring him. He wonders where Santa is now. Is he near? Finally, Frank falls asleep. 

He wakes up early on Christmas Day and rushes downstairs. His parents follow him into the living room. His stocking is bulging! And there, beneath the tree, sits a new bicycle! Frank is very happy. Santa always brings the perfect gifts.

In the afternoon, Frank's relatives gather at his grandparents' home. They all bring gifts and place them beneath the Christmas tree. They also bring foods or desserts for Christmas Dinner. Soon, the house becomes alive with wonderful aromas and happy voices. In the kitchen, his grandmother and several of his aunts and uncles are busy preparing Christmas dinner. Frank chats with his cousins. Some of them live very far away. They always have interesting stories to tell.

At last, they all have a seat at the dinner table. The table is covered with many tasty foods. There's a roasted turkey, a large ham covered with pineapples, and mashed potatoes with gravy. There are green beans, and corn, and cranberry sauce. And for desert there are delicious pudding pies, frosted sugar-cookies, and fruitcakes. It all looks so good! Frank can't decide what to try first.

After dinner, the family exchanges gifts. A smile is on every face as they open their gifts. Before opening each gift, Frank gives it a small shake. He tries to guess what's inside. Soon the floor is littered with colorful paper.

Christmas is a magical time. It is a time for being with our families. It is a time for warm songs and delicious foods. Frank wishes that Christmas could last forever.

「フランク、もうベッドに入る時間よ！」と家の中からお母さんが呼んでいます。
